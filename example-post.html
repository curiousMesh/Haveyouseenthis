Why did God ask Adam where he was?

June 2026

In Genesis 3, right after Adam and Eve eat the fruit and hide themselves, God comes walking in the garden and asks: "Where are you?"

"But the Lord God called to the man, 'Where are you?'" — Genesis 3:9

I used to read past that question without blinking. But one day it stopped me completely. God asked Adam where he was. God — who sees everything, knows everything, created everything — asked a man hiding behind some trees where he was.

He already knew. So what was the question really about?

The question wasn't for God

I think the question was for Adam. Not because God needed the information, but because Adam needed to answer it. There's something about being asked "where are you?" that forces you to locate yourself. To reckon with where you've ended up.

Adam had to say, out loud: I heard you. I was afraid. I hid. That's where I am now.

That's a completely different kind of encounter than God just finding him. The question made Adam speak his own condition. Made him name his own fear, his own shame, his own hiding.

This pattern shows up everywhere

Once I noticed it in Genesis, I started seeing it all over Scripture. God asking Elijah "What are you doing here?" under the juniper tree. Jesus asking the disciples "Who do you say that I am?" He knew. The question was for them.

There's something in the way God asks questions that is less about gathering information and more about opening a door. Giving the person a chance to say what's actually going on inside them.

I find that quietly remarkable. The first conversation after the fall wasn't an accusation. It was a question. An invitation to come out.
